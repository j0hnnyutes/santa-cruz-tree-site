Trees are some of the most valuable — and most overlooked — features of a property in Santa Cruz County. A mature coast live oak can add tens of thousands of dollars to a property's value. A dying eucalyptus or a redwood with root problems can represent an enormous liability. An arborist report documents exactly what you have, what condition it's in, and what you're responsible for.

Whether you're buying, selling, refinancing, or simply want documentation for insurance or permit purposes, here's what an arborist report covers and when you need one.

## What Is an Arborist Report?

An arborist report (also called a tree survey, tree assessment, or tree risk assessment) is a written evaluation of one or more trees prepared by an ISA Certified Arborist. The report documents:

- **Tree species, size, and location** on the property
- **Structural condition:** trunk integrity, branch attachment, root zone, any visible decay or damage
- **Health assessment:** canopy vigor, signs of disease or pest activity, drought stress
- **Risk rating:** likelihood of failure and potential consequences if failure occurs
- **Recommendations:** pruning, cabling, monitoring, or removal as appropriate

Reports are written documents with photos, diagrams, and the arborist's credentials and signature. They hold up in real estate transactions, insurance claims, and permit applications.

## When a Real Estate Transaction Requires an Arborist Report

**When buying a home in Santa Cruz County**, an arborist report is not legally required — but it's often strongly advisable. A standard home inspection typically gives trees a passing glance; a general inspector is not trained to evaluate structural integrity, disease, or root problems. The inspector will note obvious dead trees or damaged limbs, but they won't assess whether the large eucalyptus leaning toward the master bedroom is structurally sound.

**When selling,** a proactive arborist report can work in your favor — it documents the condition of valuable trees, reduces buyer uncertainty, and can be used to justify the tree-related aspects of the listing price. Some sellers use arborist reports to get ahead of issues that might come up in negotiation.

**Lender requirements:** FHA and VA loans sometimes require evidence that significant tree hazards have been addressed before a loan will fund. If the home inspection notes a "large dead tree near the structure," expect the lender to require documentation.

**Title companies and escrow:** In Santa Cruz County, tree disputes and tree-related property damage claims occasionally surface during escrow. An arborist report is the authoritative document used to assess and resolve these questions.

## When a Permit Requires an Arborist Report

Many jurisdictions in Santa Cruz County require an arborist report as part of the tree removal permit application:

- **City of Santa Cruz:** Required for heritage tree removal applications
- **Santa Cruz County (unincorporated):** Required for protected species removal in designated zones
- **Capitola, Scotts Valley, Watsonville:** Check current municipal requirements — ordinances vary

The report documents why removal is recommended (typically hazard, disease, or structural failure), supporting the permit application with professional documentation.

## What an Arborist Report Costs in Santa Cruz

Arborist report fees in Santa Cruz County typically range:

- **Single tree assessment:** $200–$400
- **Property survey (up to 10 trees):** $400–$800
- **Large property or complex assessment (10+ trees):** $800–$2,000+

The fee covers the arborist's on-site time, photo documentation, and the written report. For real estate transactions, this cost is typically far outweighed by the property value at stake or the cost of a missed risk.

Some contractors include a basic written assessment with their removal estimate at no additional charge — but a permit-quality or litigation-quality arborist report is a more formal document with more thorough documentation.

## What to Look for in an Arborist Report

A quality arborist report should include:

**ISA credentials.** The author should be an ISA Certified Arborist, and the credential number should appear in the report. Verify it at isa-arbor.com/verify.

**Photos.** The report should include photographs of each evaluated tree, including close-ups of any areas of concern.

**TRAQ methodology.** The Tree Risk Assessment Qualification (TRAQ) is the ISA-developed standard framework for tree risk assessment. Reports prepared using TRAQ methodology are the recognized standard for professional and legal purposes.

**Clear recommendations.** The report should specify exactly what is recommended — pruning specifications, monitoring intervals, or removal recommendation with supporting reasoning.

**Site plan or map.** For multi-tree reports, a diagram showing tree locations on the property helps contextualize the findings.

## Common Issues Arborist Reports Uncover in Santa Cruz County

In our experience evaluating properties throughout Santa Cruz County, the most common issues arborist reports document:

**Eucalyptus near structures.** Blue gum eucalyptus is the most commonly problematic tree in pre-purchase inspections — large, structurally complex, and frequently positioned close to homes.

**Sudden Oak Death.** Active SOD infection or advanced early-stage infection is found in coast live oaks throughout the Santa Cruz Mountains and coastal areas.

**Root intrusion.** Redwood, eucalyptus, and fig roots actively impacting foundations, sewer lines, or drainage infrastructure.

**Storm damage history.** Cracks, included bark unions, and old wound zones not visible from the ground that represent failure risks.

**Protected species surprises.** Mature trees that turn out to be subject to local protection ordinances — requiring permits the seller didn't know they'd need before the sale can close.
