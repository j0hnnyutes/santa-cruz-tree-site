Eucalyptus trees are everywhere in Santa Cruz County — lining canyon roads, towering over backyards, standing sentinel over hillside neighborhoods. Blue gum eucalyptus (*Eucalyptus globulus*) was planted extensively throughout California in the late 1800s and early 1900s, and the trees that remain today are massive, fast-growing, and in many cases overdue for professional attention.

If you have a eucalyptus on your property — or near it — this guide covers everything you need to know: why they're problematic, how removal works, what it costs in Santa Cruz, and when a permit is required.

## Why Eucalyptus Trees Are Different

Eucalyptus aren't native to California, and their biology is fundamentally different from the oaks, redwoods, and bay laurels that evolved here. A few key differences that affect removal:

**Size and growth rate.** Blue gum eucalyptus routinely reach 150–200 feet in Santa Cruz County. They grow fast — sometimes three to six feet per year when young — and by the time a property owner notices a problem, the tree may already be enormous.

**Wood behavior.** Eucalyptus wood is unpredictable. It can crack, split, and shed large limbs without warning — a phenomenon known as "sudden limb drop" or "summer branch drop." Unlike most trees that show structural weakness before failure, eucalyptus can drop limbs from an apparently healthy-looking canopy.

**Fire risk.** Eucalyptus is one of the most flammable trees in California. The bark sheds in long strips that accumulate at the base and ignite easily. The leaves contain volatile oils that cause the tree to burn intensely. For properties in Santa Cruz County's Wildland-Urban Interface (WUI) — Ben Lomond, Scotts Valley, the Santa Cruz Mountains — eucalyptus near structures is a significant fire hazard.

**Root system.** Eucalyptus has an extensive, shallow root system that can lift sidewalks, crack foundations, and interfere with underground utilities. Roots can extend two to three times the canopy radius.

## When to Remove a Eucalyptus Tree

Not every eucalyptus needs to come down — some are structurally sound, appropriately located, and can be managed with regular maintenance. But removal is worth serious consideration when:

- The tree is within falling distance of a house, outbuilding, or frequently occupied area
- The tree is in a fire-hazard zone and poses defensible-space concerns
- You're seeing significant bark loss, crown dieback, or leaning that has worsened over time
- Large limbs have already dropped from the tree
- The root system is actively damaging hardscape, utilities, or neighboring structures
- The tree is dead, dying, or has suffered major wind or storm damage

If you're unsure, a hazard assessment from a certified arborist will give you a documented evaluation of the tree's structural condition and risk level.

## Eucalyptus Removal Cost in Santa Cruz County

Eucalyptus removal is among the most expensive tree removal work in our region, for good reason: the trees are large, the wood is heavy and unpredictable, and the jobs often require specialized rigging and cranes.

Typical eucalyptus removal costs in Santa Cruz County:

- **Medium eucalyptus (50–80 feet):** $1,500–$3,000
- **Large eucalyptus (80–120 feet):** $3,000–$6,000
- **Very large eucalyptus (120+ feet near structures):** $5,000–$12,000+

Crane use is common for large removals, especially those near structures. Crane rentals add $800–$1,500/day, but they dramatically reduce risk and often make the job faster. For very large trees, the crane cost is often offset by reduced labor time.

Additional cost factors:
- **Stump grinding:** $150–$400 depending on stump diameter
- **Debris hauling:** Usually included; confirm with your contractor
- **Multiple trees:** Volume discounts often apply when removing several trees at once

## Does Removing a Eucalyptus Require a Permit in Santa Cruz?

This depends on your specific jurisdiction:

**City of Santa Cruz:** Trees over a certain diameter require a Tree Removal Permit from the Planning Department. Eucalyptus are generally not protected species, but permit requirements apply based on size, not species.

**Santa Cruz County (unincorporated areas):** The County does not currently require permits for eucalyptus removal on private property, but this can vary based on location within the WUI or if the property is subject to a specific overlay zone.

**Capitola, Scotts Valley, Watsonville:** Each municipality has its own ordinance. Always check with your local planning department before removing a large tree.

## Fire Safety and Defensible Space

Under California's defensible space law (PRC 4291), property owners in State Responsibility Areas must maintain 100 feet of defensible space around structures. Eucalyptus trees within that zone present specific challenges:

- Accumulated bark litter and dead leaf debris should be cleaned up regularly
- Lower limbs ("ladder fuels") should be removed up to 6–10 feet from the ground
- Trees within Zone 1 (0–30 feet from structure) that pose direct ignition risk should be evaluated for removal

CAL FIRE and local fire departments increasingly recommend removing eucalyptus within close proximity to homes in fire-prone areas of Santa Cruz County — particularly Ben Lomond, Boulder Creek, Felton, and Scotts Valley.

## What Eucalyptus Removal Looks Like

Large eucalyptus removals are typically multi-day jobs involving a crew of 3–5 people. The process:

1. **Site assessment:** The lead arborist evaluates the tree's lean, root zone, and surrounding hazards, then plans the rigging and drop sequence.
2. **Crane setup (if applicable):** A crane is positioned to receive and control large sections of trunk and major limbs.
3. **Top-down removal:** Working from the top down, the climber or crew removes the canopy in sections. Each piece is lowered on rigging lines to prevent uncontrolled falls.
4. **Trunk sections:** The trunk is cut in sections from top to bottom and lowered by crane or rigging.
5. **Stump and cleanup:** The stump is ground out, chips are hauled or blown to the curb for pickup, and the site is cleaned.

For very large trees (120+ feet), the job may take two full days with a crane and large crew.

## Eucalyptus Wood: What to Do With It

Eucalyptus wood is dense, aromatic, and burns hot — it makes excellent firewood once properly seasoned (eucalyptus needs at least a year to season; green wood is difficult to burn and produces more smoke). If you want to keep the wood from your removal, let the crew know in advance. They can section the trunk into firewood rounds and leave them on-site.

Chips from the canopy and smaller branches can be used as mulch, though eucalyptus mulch decomposes slowly and may have allelopathic effects (inhibiting growth of some plants). It's generally not recommended directly around vegetable gardens.
