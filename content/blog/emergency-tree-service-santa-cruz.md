A tree comes down at 2 a.m. during a winter storm. It's on your roof. Or blocking your driveway. Or dangling over a power line. What do you do?

Emergency tree situations happen more often in Santa Cruz than most people expect. Our winter storms are no joke — the combination of saturated soil, high winds, and mature tree canopy creates real hazards every year. Here's exactly what to do when you're facing an emergency, what to expect from a tree service, and what it's going to cost.

## Step 1: Safety First — Before Anything Else

**If a tree or limb has hit a power line, do not go near it.** Call PG&E immediately at 1-800-743-5000 and stay well clear until they confirm the line is de-energized. Downed lines can energize the ground around them — the danger zone is larger than it looks.

**If the tree has hit your home:** Stay out of the affected area until it's assessed. A tree on a roof can compromise the structure in ways that aren't obvious from outside. Don't walk through rooms below the impact point until a professional says it's safe.

**If the tree is blocking a road or driveway:** Call 911 if it's a public road hazard. For your own driveway, a tree service can typically get to it faster than you'd expect — more on response times below.

**Document everything before cleanup begins.** Take photos and video of the tree in its fallen position, the damage to structures, and the surrounding area. This is critical for insurance claims. Don't let anyone remove anything until you've documented it thoroughly.

## Step 2: Call Your Insurance Company

If a tree has fallen on an insured structure — your house, garage, fence, or another covered structure — your homeowners insurance **typically covers the removal** and the structural damage repairs, minus your deductible. Call your insurer before authorizing major work so the claim is properly opened.

Note: if a tree falls in your yard but doesn't hit a structure, insurance usually won't cover removal. That's just a cleanup cost.

## Step 3: Call a Tree Service

Reputable emergency tree services in Santa Cruz operate 24/7. Response times vary — in the immediate aftermath of a major storm, every company in the area gets slammed at once. Expect longer waits right after a big storm event. If the situation isn't immediately dangerous, sometimes waiting until morning is the practical choice.

When you call, be specific:
- Is the tree on a structure, vehicle, or power line?
- Is anyone injured or trapped?
- Approximate size of the tree
- Your address and best way to access the property

## What Emergency Tree Removal Costs in 2025

Emergency tree removal runs **$879 to $6,000** for most residential situations, with pricing heavily influenced by:

**Tree size:**
- Small trees (under 30 ft): $300–$800
- Medium trees (30–60 ft): $800–$1,800
- Large trees (60–80 ft): $1,500–$3,000
- Extra-large or complex: $2,000–$5,000+

**The emergency premium:** Expect to pay **25–50% more** than a scheduled removal for after-hours or same-day response — and potentially **50–100% more** in the immediate aftermath of a widespread storm event when demand surges. This is standard across the industry and reflects real costs: crew overtime, weekend rates, and mobilizing equipment quickly.

**Additional cost factors:**
- Tree on a structure or vehicle (requires crane, more careful work)
- Multiple large limbs or sections requiring crane lifts
- Limited access (steep hillside, narrow driveway)
- Stump grinding (often quoted separately)
- Debris hauling (confirm it's included)

## What About the Stump?

Emergency removal typically addresses the hazard — the fallen tree, the dangerous lean, the dangling limb. Stump grinding is usually a separate service scheduled for a follow-up visit. If you want it done at the same time, ask for it in the initial quote.

## After the Emergency: What to Do Next

Once the immediate hazard is dealt with, take stock:

**Inspect your remaining trees.** A storm that takes one tree rarely leaves the others untouched. Look for cracked or hanging limbs, newly exposed root zones from saturated soil, and any trees that are suddenly leaning that weren't before.

**Don't ignore partial damage.** A large broken limb that's "hung up" in the canopy — what arborists call a "widow maker" — is one of the most dangerous situations in residential tree work. It looks stable until it isn't.

**Get a professional assessment.** After any significant storm, a walk-around assessment by an experienced arborist can identify hazards you might not notice. Many companies offer this at low or no cost.

## Signs You Have a Tree Emergency

Not every tree problem is an emergency — but some things warrant a same-day call:

- A tree or large limb is on or touching your home, power line, or vehicle
- A large limb is hanging partially broken ("widow maker")
- A tree is significantly leaning after a storm or in saturated soil
- You can see a large crack running through the main trunk or a major scaffold branch
- Roots are visibly lifted on one side (the tree is starting to uproot)
- The tree is near an occupied structure and showing signs of imminent failure

When in doubt, call. The cost of an assessment is far lower than the cost of waiting.

## Santa Cruz's Specific Storm Risks

Santa Cruz County sees extended rain events from October through April, atmospheric rivers that can dump inches of rain in hours, and periodic high-wind events that follow storms when the ground is already saturated. Mature trees — especially shallow-rooted species like eucalyptus and some planted ornamentals — are at highest risk in these conditions.

If you have large trees near your home and haven't had them assessed recently, the best time to do it is before storm season, not after.
