Santa Cruz County is home to some of the most majestic trees in California — redwoods, oaks, and eucalyptus that have been growing for decades or even centuries. But trees in an urban and suburban environment require management, and some situations create genuine risks to structures, vehicles, and people.

Here are the five most common hazardous tree situations we see throughout the county — and what to do about each.

## 1. Mature Eucalyptus Over Structures

Blue gum eucalyptus (*Eucalyptus globulus*) is everywhere in Santa Cruz County — planted extensively in the 19th and early 20th centuries as a timber crop that never quite panned out. These trees can grow to 150 feet or more, and they have some characteristics that make them uniquely dangerous in a residential setting:

**They drop limbs without warning.** Eucalyptus is notorious for what arborists call "sudden limb failure" — large branches breaking and falling on calm, warm days with no storm or obvious stress. This happens because the wood is dense and heavy, branches grow rapidly and sometimes ahead of the trunk's structural development, and internal decay can be difficult to detect visually.

**The root systems are aggressive.** Eucalyptus roots extend far beyond the canopy drip line and can affect foundations, underground utilities, and drain lines.

**The fire risk is significant.** As discussed in our fire-resistant landscaping guide, eucalyptus litter, bark, and foliage create serious fuel loads near homes.

**What to do:** Have mature eucalyptus near structures assessed by a certified arborist. Regular canopy thinning, deadwood removal, and crown raising (limbing up) reduces risk. In many cases near homes, removal is the responsible choice.

## 2. Trees with Root Damage or Compromised Anchoring

A tree's stability depends on its root system. But roots are underground and out of sight, so root damage often goes unnoticed until the tree fails.

Common causes of root damage in Santa Cruz County include:

- **Construction activity:** Trenching for utilities, grading, compaction from heavy equipment — all of these can kill substantial portions of a tree's root system. A tree can look healthy for 2–5 years after significant root damage before the effects become visible.
- **Soil compaction:** Repeated vehicle traffic or hardscape installed over root zones compresses soil, cutting off oxygen and water to roots.
- **Fungal root rot:** Several pathogens — including *Armillaria* (honey fungus) and *Phytophthora* — attack root systems, causing decay that's invisible from the surface.

**Signs of compromised anchoring:**
- Visible fungal growth (mushrooms) at the base of the trunk
- Soil heaving or cracking on one side of the tree
- A tree that has developed a new lean
- Sparse canopy despite otherwise good conditions

**What to do:** Any tree showing these signs should be assessed promptly by a professional. Root failure is typically sudden, and a falling tree with a root ball attached does enormous damage.

## 3. Trees with Included Bark at Major Unions

An *included bark union* occurs when two branches — or a branch and the trunk — grow so closely together that bark is trapped between them rather than forming a strong wood-to-wood connection. The result is an inherently weak junction that can fail under load, especially during wind and rain.

Included bark often looks like a tight "V" where two branches meet, with a thin line of dark bark visible in the crevice. A healthy union looks more like a "U" with a raised ridge of bark (the branch bark ridge) outside the junction.

This is one of the most underappreciated hazards in residential trees. Many trees have one or more large co-dominant stems that are joined by included bark — and this union can fail catastrophically when the tree is 30 or 40 years old, taking out a section that was never expected to fail.

**What to do:** Young trees with included bark unions can sometimes be corrected through structural pruning or cabling. Older trees should be assessed to determine whether the union is stable and whether cabling or removal is warranted.

## 4. Over-Extended Limbs Above High-Value Targets

Even a healthy, structurally sound tree can pose risk if it has very long, heavy horizontal limbs growing directly over a house, car, or deck. Over time, these limbs develop a natural end-weight that increases the stress on the branch union. Add the dynamic loading of wind, snow load (in the mountains), or a wet California winter, and the math changes.

This is especially common with oak trees, which often develop long, sweeping horizontal branches, and with figs and other trees planted close to homes decades ago.

**What to do:** Assess the trajectory and end-weight of major limbs over structures. End-weight reduction (removing the outermost portion of a heavy limb) and cabling can often manage the risk without removing the entire tree. In some cases, removal of specific branches is the right call.

## 5. Dead or Severely Declining Trees

A dead tree is essentially a waiting hazard. As wood decays, it loses structural strength unpredictably — a tree that seems solid may have extensive internal rot invisible from the outside. Root decay often accompanies trunk decay, further compromising anchoring.

In Santa Cruz County, particularly common causes of tree death include:

- **Sudden Oak Death (SOD):** A devastating disease caused by *Phytophthora ramorum* that has killed millions of oaks across coastal California, particularly tanoak.
- **Drought stress:** Extended drought weakens trees and makes them susceptible to secondary pests and pathogens.
- **Bark beetle infestation:** Bark beetles commonly attack drought-stressed or already-dying trees, accelerating their decline.
- **Root rot pathogens:** *Armillaria* and other root pathogens can kill trees while leaving the trunk appearing intact.

**What to do:** Dead trees near structures or pedestrian areas should be removed. The urgency depends on the tree's size, proximity to targets, and how advanced the decay is — but there's no improvement over time. A dead tree only gets worse.
