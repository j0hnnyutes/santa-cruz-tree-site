Homeowners associations add a significant layer of complexity to tree decisions in Santa Cruz County. The city or county ordinance isn't the only rulebook in play — your CC&Rs (Covenants, Conditions & Restrictions), the HOA's governing documents, and California civil code all intersect when you want to trim a tree, remove a hazard, or resolve a dispute over a tree that's damaging your property.

This guide covers the most common HOA tree situations Santa Cruz County homeowners face and how California law and typical HOA documents address them.

*This is general information, not legal advice. For specific disputes or significant property damage situations, consult a California licensed attorney familiar with HOA and property law.*

## Who "Owns" the Trees in an HOA Community?

The first step in any HOA tree situation is determining ownership. In a typical California HOA community, trees fall into one of three categories:

**Common area trees.** Trees in shared spaces — parks, greenbelts, entry areas, along shared roads — are HOA property. The HOA is responsible for their maintenance, care, and liability if they cause damage through negligence.

**Exclusive use common area.** Some HOA communities designate the landscaping immediately adjacent to or within a homeowner's unit or lot as "exclusive use common area." This means the HOA owns the tree, but the homeowner has exclusive use of the surrounding area. Maintenance responsibility depends on what the CC&Rs specify — it's often split between HOA (structural care, removal) and homeowner (routine maintenance, irrigation).

**Lot trees.** Trees on a homeowner's individually owned lot are typically the homeowner's property and responsibility — unless the CC&Rs say otherwise.

**Read your CC&Rs.** The specific language in your community's governing documents controls. Generic rules don't apply when your CC&Rs say something different.

## Can the HOA Make You Remove (or Keep) a Tree?

Yes — to both.

**HOAs can require removal.** If your CC&Rs include architectural standards or nuisance provisions, and a tree on your lot violates those standards (blocking sightlines, creating excessive debris for neighbors, posing a documented hazard), the HOA board may have authority to require removal or trimming. They typically must follow a notice-and-hearing process before compelling action.

**HOAs can prevent removal.** Conversely, if your CC&Rs protect certain trees or require approval for significant landscaping changes, the HOA may have the authority to prevent you from removing a tree even if you want to. Architectural review committees frequently have approval authority over significant landscaping changes, including tree removal.

**The HOA's authority has limits.** If a tree is a documented safety hazard — certified by an ISA arborist, confirmed by an independent assessment — and the HOA refuses to allow or perform removal, the homeowner or board has a potential negligence liability issue. California courts have found that HOA boards can be liable for damages caused by hazard trees on common areas that the board knew about and failed to address.

## When a Common Area Tree Damages Your Property

This is one of the most frequent tree disputes in HOA communities. A tree from the greenbelt drops a large limb on your fence, roof, or car. Who pays?

**The HOA is generally responsible for common area trees.** Under California law, property owners (including HOAs) can be liable for damages caused by trees they own if they knew or should have known the tree was hazardous. If the tree was healthy and its failure was unforeseeable, the HOA typically is not liable, and your homeowner's insurance is your primary recourse.

**Documentation is everything.** If you previously notified the HOA in writing about a hazardous tree — dead branches, visible decay, leaning that wasn't there before — and the HOA failed to act, you have a strong basis for liability. If you never notified them, proving they "should have known" is harder.

**What to do:** After an incident involving an HOA common area tree:
1. Document the damage and the fallen tree or branch thoroughly with photos
2. Report to the HOA board in writing immediately
3. Notify your homeowner's insurance
4. Keep all correspondence with the HOA
5. Get an arborist's written assessment of the tree's condition before it's cleaned up

## Trimming Branches That Overhang From HOA Common Areas

California law gives you the right to trim branches that overhang onto your property, up to the property line — this applies even when the tree belongs to the HOA. You pay for the trimming and must not harm the tree.

However, many CC&Rs restrict homeowners from performing work in common areas or from hiring their own contractors to touch HOA property without board approval. Read your CC&Rs before proceeding, and if in doubt, request in writing that the HOA address the overhanging branches. This both creates documentation and gives the HOA the opportunity to manage their own tree first.

## Tree Approval for Home Improvement Projects

If you're planning a project that involves removing trees on your lot — a pool, an addition, new hardscape — most HOA architectural review processes require approval of any significant landscaping changes, including tree removal.

Key considerations:
- Submit the architectural review application with arborist documentation of any trees being removed
- Allow adequate time for HOA review (often 30–60 days per the CC&Rs)
- Check whether the CC&Rs require replacement planting
- Confirm whether city or county permits are also required independently of HOA approval

HOA approval does not replace city or county tree removal permits. You may need both.

## What Happens When Neighbors Disagree About an HOA Tree

Tree disputes between neighbors within HOA communities often get routed to the HOA board as a "neighbor dispute." The board's ability to resolve these varies:

**If the tree is in common area:** The board has clear authority and responsibility to assess and address the issue.

**If the tree is on a private lot:** The board typically has authority only if there's a CC&R provision being violated (nuisance, architectural standard, documented hazard). They can't usually force a homeowner to act on a tree that isn't violating any governing document provision.

**Alternative dispute resolution:** California HOA law (Civil Code 5925) requires HOAs to make available internal dispute resolution and alternative dispute resolution (ADR, such as mediation) processes for member disputes, including neighbor-to-neighbor issues. This is often a faster and less expensive path than litigation.

## Getting an Arborist Report for HOA Disputes

In most HOA tree disputes — whether you're trying to compel the HOA to address a hazard, respond to an HOA request to remove your tree, or document damage for insurance — a written arborist report from an ISA Certified Arborist is the foundational document. It establishes:

- The tree's species, size, and ownership (which side of the property line the trunk is on)
- Its structural condition and health
- Any documented hazard risk
- Professional recommendations for action

This report gives the HOA board, your insurance company, or a mediator a professional, objective basis for decision-making. It also protects you: if you've obtained and provided an arborist report documenting a hazard, you've fulfilled your notification obligation, and the failure to act shifts to whoever you notified.
