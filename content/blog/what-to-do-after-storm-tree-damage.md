Santa Cruz County gets battered by Pacific storms every winter — high winds, heavy rain, and occasionally heavy snow in the mountains. These storms regularly cause tree failures: broken limbs, uprooted trees, trunk splits, and canopy damage that ranges from cosmetic to catastrophic.

If you're dealing with storm-damaged trees on your property, this guide will walk you through what to do safely and effectively.

## Step 1: Stay Back — Assess from a Distance First

Before you touch anything, take a careful look from a safe distance. Storm-damaged trees can be deceptively unstable. A tree that appears to be holding can shift suddenly, and a branch under tension can snap with tremendous force.

Look for:

- **Hanging limbs ("widow makers"):** Large broken branches still partially attached are extremely dangerous. They can fall with no warning and cause serious injury.
- **Uprooted trees:** A tree that has tipped but is held up by its canopy or surrounding trees can come down when the conditions change.
- **Power line contact:** If a fallen tree or branch is touching utility lines, stay well clear and call your utility company immediately. Do not touch the tree, the lines, or anything in contact with either.
- **Structural contact:** If a tree or large branch has fallen on your house, do not go inside the building until a structural inspector has assessed it. Fallen trees can compromise roofs, walls, and foundations in ways that aren't immediately visible.

## Step 2: Document Everything for Insurance

Before any cleanup begins, document all damage thoroughly with photos and video. Walk the entire perimeter if safe to do so. Capture:

- All damaged or fallen trees
- Any property damage (roof, fence, structures)
- The position and size of fallen limbs

Your homeowner's insurance may cover removal of trees that fall on structures or block access to your property. Documentation is essential for filing a claim. Check your policy — many policies have specific language about which scenarios are covered.

## Step 3: Prioritize Safety Hazards

Not all storm damage is equally urgent. Prioritize issues in this order:

**Immediate emergencies (same day):**
- Trees or branches on power lines — call the utility company
- Trees that have fallen on or are leaning against the house
- Hanging limbs directly over pedestrian paths, driveways, or play areas
- Trees showing sudden lean that didn't exist before the storm

**High priority (within days):**
- Large broken or partially attached limbs that haven't fallen yet
- Uprooted trees that are unstable
- Trees with significant trunk splits

**Lower priority:**
- Broken smaller branches with no structural risk
- Trees with canopy damage but stable trunks and roots
- Stumps from trees that fell cleanly

## Step 4: Don't Attempt Dangerous Removal Yourself

The most common mistake after storm damage is homeowners trying to cut down damaged trees or remove large hanging branches themselves. Storm-damaged trees are under unpredictable tension. Cutting the wrong branch — or cutting it in the wrong direction — can cause it to spring back violently or fall in an unexpected direction.

Leave the following to professionals:

- Any branch larger than your wrist in diameter that is hanging or broken
- Any tree that is leaning significantly
- Any tree with damage at the base or roots
- Any work that requires a chainsaw above shoulder height

It's fine to pick up and dispose of small fallen branches and debris yourself — that's reasonable cleanup. But significant cuts should be done by someone trained in storm damage response.

## Step 5: Call an Arborist for Assessment

After an immediate safety hazard has been addressed, have a certified arborist evaluate all trees that sustained visible damage. A tree that lost a large limb may look fine from the outside, but that wound is an entry point for disease and decay. An arborist can tell you:

- Whether the remaining structure is sound
- Whether the tree is worth saving with proper pruning
- Whether the tree is a continuing safety risk and should come down
- What to expect from the tree's recovery

Many trees can be saved after storm damage with proper pruning and care. The key is acting quickly before decay takes hold in open wounds.

## What Arborists Do After Storm Damage

Storm damage response typically involves:

**Hazard removal:** Cutting and removing hanging limbs, stabilizing or removing leaning trees, clearing fallen trees from structures and access points.

**Crown cleanup:** Removing torn, broken, or dangling branches that remain in the canopy. Proper pruning cuts at the right points encourage the tree to seal over the wound and recover.

**Structural assessment:** Evaluating whether the remaining tree is structurally sound and whether it poses ongoing risk to the property.

**Cable and bracing (where appropriate):** For trees with partial splits or significant structural damage, cables and braces can sometimes be installed to stabilize the tree for the long term.

## After the Emergency: Tree Recovery

Trees are remarkably resilient, but they do need time and ideally some support after significant storm damage.

- **Watering:** Stressed trees benefit from consistent soil moisture during recovery, especially if root damage occurred
- **Avoid fertilizing immediately:** Pushing top growth on a stressed tree can work against recovery
- **Monitor for disease:** Open wounds can invite fungal and bacterial infections; an arborist can apply protective treatments on significant cuts
- **Give it a season:** Don't judge a storm-damaged tree's recovery until you've seen a full growing season. Some trees that look severely damaged can bounce back remarkably well.
