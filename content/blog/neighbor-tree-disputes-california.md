Few things stir up more conflict between neighbors than trees. Overhanging branches. Roots cracking a driveway. Leaves clogging gutters. A looming dead tree that you're terrified will fall on your roof. If you're a Santa Cruz County homeowner dealing with any of these situations, understanding California's tree laws can help you protect your rights, resolve conflicts properly, and avoid expensive legal mistakes.

*Note: This article provides general information about California tree law as commonly applied. It is not legal advice. For disputes involving significant property damage or legal liability, consult a licensed California attorney.*

## Who Owns a Tree on the Property Line?

In California, **a tree belongs to the property where its trunk originates** — specifically, where the center of the trunk is located at ground level.

If the trunk is entirely on your neighbor's property, it's their tree — even if branches and roots extend onto your land. If the trunk sits exactly on the property line (boundary tree), California courts have generally held that both property owners share ownership of the tree, and neither can remove it without the other's consent.

## Can You Cut Your Neighbor's Overhanging Branches?

Yes — in California, you have the right to **trim branches and roots that encroach onto your property**, up to the property line. This is called the "self-help" remedy. You can do this without your neighbor's permission.

**Important limitations:**
- You may only trim up to the property line — you cannot cross onto your neighbor's property to do the work
- You must not harm the tree in the process. If your trimming kills or substantially damages the tree, you may be liable for the tree's value
- You are responsible for the cost of trimming that encroaches branches or roots
- In some cities and counties, even trimming on your own property may require permits if the work is significant enough

Before you trim, it's worth a conversation with your neighbor. In most cases, a simple discussion resolves things. If the overhanging branches are significant, you may be able to ask the neighbor to take care of them — or split the cost.

## Is Your Neighbor Responsible If Their Tree Falls on Your Property?

This is the question we hear most often, and the answer is nuanced:

**Generally, no — if the tree was healthy.** California law follows the principle that a property owner is not automatically liable for damage caused by a healthy tree that falls during a storm or due to natural causes. Acts of nature are considered outside the owner's control.

**Yes, if the owner knew (or should have known) the tree was hazardous.** This is the critical exception. If a tree was dead, visibly declining, structurally compromised, or otherwise clearly dangerous, and the owner failed to address it, they can be found liable for resulting property damage under a negligence theory.

The practical implication: **if you have documentation that you notified your neighbor about a hazardous tree, in writing, before it caused damage, you significantly strengthen a liability claim.** Send a certified letter or email. Attach photos. Hire an arborist to produce a written report documenting the hazard.

## What to Do If You're Concerned About a Neighbor's Tree

1. **Document the condition.** Take clear photographs of the tree — the trunk, any visible decay, dead wood, lean, and proximity to your structure.

2. **Get an arborist report.** An ISA Certified Arborist can provide a written assessment of the tree's condition and risk level. This document is important if you need to pursue legal action later.

3. **Notify your neighbor in writing.** Send a certified letter (or at minimum, a timestamped email) explaining your concern and requesting the neighbor address the issue. Keep a copy.

4. **Contact your homeowner's insurance.** Your insurer can advise on what would be covered if the tree falls and whether they have mechanisms for pursuing the neighbor's insurer.

5. **Escalate if necessary.** If the neighbor is unresponsive and the tree is clearly hazardous, you may have grounds for a nuisance claim. Consult a California attorney who handles property law.

## What If Your Tree Falls on a Neighbor's Property?

If your healthy tree falls on a neighbor's property during a storm, **you are generally not liable** for the damage under California law. The neighbor's homeowner's insurance typically covers the damage to their structure.

However, you *are* responsible for:
- Clearing the debris from the neighbor's property (the tree is yours)
- Any damage caused if the tree was in a hazardous condition you knew about

Your homeowner's insurance may cover your liability if the tree was found to be your responsibility — review your policy and contact your insurer promptly after any incident.

## Tree Ordinances and Permit Disputes

Santa Cruz County's various jurisdictions all have tree protection ordinances that can affect what you and your neighbor can legally do with trees:

**City of Santa Cruz:** Heritage trees (meeting certain diameter thresholds) cannot be removed without a permit. Unpermitted removal carries significant fines.

**Capitola:** Protected tree ordinance — check with city planning before any significant pruning or removal.

**Scotts Valley:** Has a tree removal permit process for regulated trees.

**Unincorporated Santa Cruz County:** County code applies — species-specific protections and size thresholds determine permit requirements.

If your neighbor removes a protected tree without a permit, report it to the local planning or code enforcement department. Fines can be substantial and the neighbor may be required to replace the tree.

Conversely, if you need to remove a tree that's threatening your property, make sure you understand the permit requirements before starting work — even a hazardous tree may require documentation and a permit.

## When a Tree Dispute Ends in Court

Tree disputes that can't be resolved between neighbors sometimes end up in civil court. Common claims include:

**Nuisance:** A tree or its parts (roots, branches, fruit, debris) that unreasonably interferes with your use and enjoyment of your property. California courts have found nuisance liability in cases involving persistent root intrusion, ongoing structural damage, and documented hazardous conditions.

**Trespass:** Technically, encroaching roots and branches can be considered trespass to property. Courts vary in how they treat this.

**Negligence:** The most common basis for recovering damages when a neighbor's tree causes property damage — requires showing the neighbor knew or should have known about the hazard.

**Timber trespass (Civil Code 3346):** If a neighbor removes your tree without permission, this statute allows recovery of double or triple the tree's value.

Having a professional arborist report is typically essential to any tree-related litigation — it establishes the tree's condition, value, and risk level.
