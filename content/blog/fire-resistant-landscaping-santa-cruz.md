Santa Cruz County has seen firsthand what wildfires can do. The CZU Lightning Complex fire in 2020 burned over 86,000 acres across Santa Cruz and San Mateo counties, destroying hundreds of homes. For residents in the mountains, foothills, and wildland-urban interface — Boulder Creek, Ben Lomond, Felton, Scotts Valley, and parts of Santa Cruz itself — fire-resistant landscaping isn't just a nice-to-have. It can be the difference between a home that survives and one that doesn't.

## What Is Defensible Space?

Defensible space is the buffer you create between your home and the vegetation that could fuel a wildfire. California law requires homeowners in State Responsibility Areas (SRA) to maintain defensible space of at least 100 feet around structures.

The defensible space is divided into two zones:

- **Zone 1 (0–30 feet from your home):** Lean, Clean, and Green. Remove all dead or dying vegetation, keep grass mowed short, and thin out trees so branches don't touch each other or the house. No plants directly against your house.
- **Zone 2 (30–100 feet from your home):** Reduce fuel. Cut or mow grass to 4 inches or less, remove dead plants, and create spacing between plants and trees so fire can't easily travel.

## Trees and Shrubs to Avoid Near Your Home

Some species are significantly more flammable than others. In Santa Cruz County, these are the most common high-fire-risk trees and shrubs that homeowners should be cautious about planting — or should remove or heavily manage near structures:

### Eucalyptus

Eucalyptus is beautiful and iconic along the Central Coast, but it is one of the most fire-dangerous trees you can have near a home. The oily, aromatic leaves burn intensely, shed bark that creates ladder fuels, and the trees drop enormous amounts of flammable litter year-round. Burning bark from eucalyptus can travel hundreds of feet in wind, igniting new fires ahead of the main burn. If you have eucalyptus within 100 feet of your home, regular fuel reduction and limbing is essential. Many fire experts recommend full removal within the first 30 feet.

### Acacia

Several acacia species common in coastal California have oily leaves that burn readily. They also resprout aggressively after fire, creating dense thickets.

### Juniper and Arborvitae

These popular landscaping shrubs carry significant fire risk. Their dense, resinous foliage burns quickly and can torch to full height in seconds, easily spreading fire to the eaves of a home.

### Ornamental Grasses

Tall ornamental grasses dry out in late summer and become highly flammable. Keep them away from structures and cut them back in fall.

### Pine Trees

Pines drop dry, resinous needles continuously — that litter accumulates under the tree and creates excellent fuel. Keep the area under pines raked and remove low branches (this is called "limbing up") to eliminate ladder fuels.

## Fire-Resistant Trees and Plants for Santa Cruz County

Fire-resistant doesn't mean fireproof — any plant will burn under the right conditions. But these species are significantly less flammable and are good choices for Zone 1 and Zone 2 planting:

### Trees

- **Coast live oak** — An excellent native with high moisture content and relatively low flammability. Plant at appropriate spacing.
- **Toyon (Heteromeles arbutifolia)** — A California native with good fire resistance and excellent wildlife value.
- **Western redbud** — Deciduous, low canopy, not particularly flammable.
- **Olive** — Drought-tolerant with moderate fire resistance; keep limbed up and remove litter regularly.
- **Strawberry tree (Arbutus unedo)** — Good fire resistance, attractive bark, keeps moisture well.

### Shrubs and Ground Covers

- **Coyote brush (Baccharis pilularis)** — California native, moderate fire resistance, good ground cover for slopes.
- **Salvia species** — Many native salvias have reasonable fire resistance when kept irrigated and dead-headed.
- **Ice plant (Carpobrotus)** — Succulent ground cover with very high water content; excellent for slopes and fire resistance.
- **Agave** — Succulent, very high moisture content, extremely fire-resistant.
- **Rosemary** — Widely used in fire-prone areas for its low combustibility when green and irrigated.

## Tree Management Tips for Fire Safety

Even with the right species, management matters. Here are the most impactful tree-related fire safety steps:

**Limb up trees to 6–10 feet.** Removing lower branches eliminates "ladder fuels" — the pathway that lets ground fire climb into the tree canopy. This is one of the single most effective fire-safety tree maintenance steps you can take.

**Clear litter regularly.** Dead leaves, needles, bark, and small branches accumulate under trees and create ready fuel. In fire-prone areas, especially under conifers and eucalyptus, rake and remove this material at least twice a year.

**Create canopy spacing.** Trees whose canopies touch each other allow fire to travel from crown to crown. In Zone 1, space trees so canopies are at least 10 feet apart. In Zone 2, aim for 20–30 feet between canopy edges on slopes.

**Remove dead trees promptly.** Dead trees are essentially standing fuel. If you have a dead or dying tree near your home, have it removed before fire season (typically summer and fall in Santa Cruz County).

**Keep irrigation active.** Healthy, well-irrigated vegetation is significantly harder to ignite than drought-stressed plants. If you're in a fire-prone area and cutting back on irrigation in summer, reconsider the plants closest to your home.

## Working with CAL FIRE and Local Requirements

If you're in a State Responsibility Area in Santa Cruz County, CAL FIRE may inspect your property for defensible space compliance. Violations can result in fines and, more importantly, put your home at risk. CAL FIRE's website has detailed maps showing which properties fall in SRA.

Your local fire department can also provide a free inspection and specific recommendations for your property.
