If you own property in the City of Santa Cruz and you're thinking about removing a tree, you need to understand the city's tree protection ordinance before you pick up the phone and call a tree service. Removing a protected tree without a permit can result in significant fines — and in some cases, a requirement to plant replacement trees at your own expense.

This guide explains the City of Santa Cruz's tree regulations in plain language, covers what triggers a permit requirement, and tells you how to navigate the process.

*Note: Tree ordinances are amended periodically. Always confirm current requirements with the City of Santa Cruz Planning Department before proceeding with any tree removal. The information here reflects our understanding of current city policy but is not a substitute for official guidance.*

## Why Santa Cruz Has a Tree Ordinance

The City of Santa Cruz's Urban Forest Management Plan and associated tree protection ordinance exist to protect the urban forest — the collective tree canopy that provides ecological, aesthetic, economic, and public safety benefits throughout the city. Santa Cruz's tree canopy helps manage stormwater, reduces the urban heat island effect, provides wildlife habitat, and is a significant part of the city's visual character and property values.

The ordinance balances private property rights with the community interest in maintaining the urban forest. It doesn't prevent tree removal in most cases — it requires documentation and, in many cases, replacement planting.

## What Trees Are Protected Under the Santa Cruz City Ordinance

The City of Santa Cruz's tree protection ordinance covers **"Significant Trees"** — trees that meet size thresholds or are designated heritage specimens. Generally, any tree with a trunk diameter (measured at 4.5 feet above grade, called DBH — diameter at breast height) of **10 inches or more** is considered significant and triggers permit requirements for removal.

Additionally, the city designates specific trees as **Heritage Trees** based on historical significance, exceptional size, age, or unique characteristics. These receive the highest level of protection.

**Species-specific considerations:**
- **Coast live oak (*Quercus agrifolia*):** Significant oaks are strongly protected. Removal permits require documented justification (hazard, disease, structural failure).
- **Coast redwood (*Sequoia sempervirens*):** Protected, and the city takes a conservative approach to removal permits.
- **Eucalyptus:** Not a protected species per se, but size thresholds still apply. Permits may still be required based on diameter.

## When Is a Tree Removal Permit Required?

In the City of Santa Cruz, you need a Tree Removal Permit when you want to remove:
- Any significant tree (10" DBH or larger) on private property
- Any tree in the public right-of-way (street trees)
- Any heritage-designated tree regardless of size

**What about pruning?** Routine maintenance pruning (removing dead wood, clearance trimming) typically does not require a permit. However, "excessive pruning" that removes more than 25% of the live crown may be treated as partial removal and could trigger permit requirements. When in doubt, ask the Planning Department.

**Exceptions:** Permits may not be required (or may be expedited) for trees that:
- Are actively causing an imminent, documented safety hazard
- Are dead (though a dead tree still typically requires documentation)
- Are specifically exempt under the current ordinance

## How to Apply for a Tree Removal Permit in Santa Cruz

1. **Contact the Planning Department.** The City of Santa Cruz Planning Department (831-420-5100) handles tree removal permit applications. They can confirm whether your specific tree requires a permit and what documentation is needed.

2. **Submit an application.** The application typically requires:
   - Property address and owner information
   - Description of the tree (species, size, location on property)
   - Reason for removal request (hazard, disease, construction conflict, etc.)
   - An arborist report for significant trees (written assessment by an ISA Certified Arborist documenting the tree's condition and justification for removal)
   - A site plan showing the tree's location

3. **Pay the permit fee.** City permit fees for tree removal are in the range of $100–$300+ depending on the tree and circumstances.

4. **Wait for approval.** Processing times vary. For straightforward cases, permits may be approved in 1–2 weeks. Complex cases — heritage trees, contested removals, large specimens — can take longer.

5. **Comply with conditions.** Approved permits often come with conditions, most commonly a requirement to plant replacement trees. The city typically requires one or more replacement trees of a specified minimum size, planted on the property or in a nearby public location.

## Replacement Tree Requirements

The City of Santa Cruz takes replacement planting seriously. Removing a significant tree typically triggers a replacement requirement — often inch-for-inch based on the removed tree's diameter, though the specific formula varies. Replacement trees must typically be:
- A city-approved species
- A minimum specified size (often 15-gallon container or larger)
- Planted within a specified timeframe following removal
- Maintained for a minimum period (usually 2–3 years) with a survival guarantee

Your permit will specify the replacement requirements for your specific project.

## Fines for Unpermitted Tree Removal in Santa Cruz

The city takes enforcement seriously. Fines for removing a protected tree without a permit can be substantial — in some cases **three times the appraised value of the removed tree**, which for a mature coast live oak or redwood can run into tens of thousands of dollars.

Beyond the fine, the city may also require you to plant replacement trees at a ratio significantly higher than would have been required with a proper permit — sometimes triple the standard replacement requirement.

This is not a theoretical risk. The city does receive and investigate complaints about unpermitted removals, and neighbors frequently report violations.

## Permit Exemptions for Hazardous Trees

If a tree is actively creating an emergency hazard — it's fallen, it's actively failing, or a certified arborist has documented imminent failure risk — the city can move faster. In genuine emergency situations:

- Contact the Planning Department immediately and explain the emergency
- Have an ISA Certified Arborist provide written documentation of the hazard
- The city may grant emergency authorization over the phone, with paperwork to follow

Even in emergencies, document everything: photos, arborist report, communication with the city. This protects you and ensures the situation is handled on record.
