Hiring the wrong tree company is one of the more expensive mistakes a Santa Cruz homeowner can make. A bad trim can damage or kill a tree worth thousands. An uninsured crew working on your property can leave you holding liability for an injury. A "low-price" job done with bad technique can cost double to fix years later.

The good news: knowing what to look for makes it straightforward to separate legitimate professionals from the rest. Here's what actually matters.

## 1. Verify Insurance Before Anything Else

This is non-negotiable. Any tree service sending workers into your trees must carry two types of insurance:

**General liability insurance** — covers property damage if something goes wrong (a limb hits your fence, a truck rolls into your car, a branch comes through your roof during removal).

**Workers' compensation insurance** — covers crew injuries on your property. This is the one homeowners overlook. If a worker is injured on your property and the company doesn't carry workers' comp, you can be held liable. It happens.

Ask for certificates of insurance before signing anything. Legitimate companies provide them without hesitation. If a company hedges, makes excuses, or can't produce documentation, move on.

## 2. Look for ISA Certified Arborists

The title "arborist" or "tree surgeon" has no legal protection in California — anyone can use it. An **ISA Certified Arborist**, however, has passed a comprehensive exam administered by the International Society of Arboriculture and must complete ongoing continuing education to maintain the credential.

This matters practically. Certified arborists understand how trees grow, how they respond to pruning, how to identify disease and structural hazards, and how to make decisions that protect long-term tree health. Someone without this background may cut what's convenient rather than what's correct.

You can verify any arborist's certification at the ISA website (isa-arbor.com) using their credential number.

## 3. Get Multiple Written Quotes

For any significant job — a tree removal, a large trimming, or anything involving major structural work — get at least three written quotes. Not verbal estimates over the phone. Written quotes that specify:

- What work will be performed
- Which specific trees are included
- Whether debris removal and haul-away is included
- Timeline and any conditions
- Total price

The cheapest quote is not automatically the worst, and the most expensive isn't automatically the best. What you're looking for is a company that can clearly explain what they're doing and why.

If one quote is dramatically lower than the others, ask why. Sometimes the answer is legitimate. Often it reflects unlicensed workers, no insurance, or a plan to cut corners on technique.

## 4. Watch Out for These Red Flags

**"Tree topping" recommendations.** If a company recommends topping your tree — removing the main leaders and cutting the crown to large stubs — walk away. Tree topping is condemned by every major arboricultural organization. It creates structurally weak regrowth, permanently disfigures trees, often leads to tree death over time, and generates hazardous conditions. Any company that recommends it either doesn't know what they're doing or doesn't care. There is almost never a legitimate reason to top a tree.

**"Lion's-tailing."** This is the practice of stripping all the interior branches and leaves, leaving foliage only at the tips of branches. It looks dramatic but is deeply harmful — it shifts the center of gravity of the branches outward, increases wind resistance, and stresses the tree. Again, a red flag.

**Climbing spikes on live trees.** Spikes (gaffs) are appropriate for removing a dead tree but leave wounds in a live tree's vascular tissue every few inches up the trunk. A professional trimming a live tree should be using a bucket truck, aerial lift, or rope-and-saddle techniques — not spikes.

**Door-to-door solicitation after a storm.** After any major storm event in Santa Cruz, unlicensed "storm chasers" circulate through neighborhoods knocking on doors. They typically quote low, often demand cash or a large deposit upfront, do rushed substandard work, and may disappear before you notice problems. Legitimate companies are busy after storms — they're not cold-canvassing.

**Cash-only, no written contract.** Always pay by check or credit card. Cash-only transactions leave you with no recourse. A written contract gives you something to stand on if there's a dispute.

**No local presence.** Can you find real reviews? A business address? A license number to look up? Companies that are hard to verify are hard to find when something goes wrong.

## 5. Check Their Memberships and References

Membership in industry organizations — the **Tree Care Industry Association (TCIA)** or the **International Society of Arboriculture (ISA)** — signals that a company is engaged with current standards and best practices. It's not a guarantee of quality, but it's a positive signal.

References matter more than online reviews for significant jobs. Ask for references from similar projects — a large removal, a heritage tree trim, an emergency response — and actually call them. Ask specifically about whether the crew protected surrounding plants and structures, cleaned up completely, and whether there were any surprises.

## 6. Ask the Right Questions

When you're evaluating a company, a few targeted questions reveal a lot:

*"What pruning standard do you work to?"* A professional answer references ANSI A300 — the industry standard for tree care. If they've never heard of it, that's telling.

*"How will you protect my lawn and other plantings during the job?"* Professional companies use ground protection mats, move carefully, and are specific about how they'll handle access.

*"Will the same people who give me the estimate actually do the work?"* Subcontracting isn't inherently bad, but you want to know who's showing up.

*"What happens if something is damaged?"* How a company answers this question — calmly and specifically, or defensively — tells you something about how they handle problems.

## 7. Understand What You're Paying For

Tree work is skilled, physical, hazardous labor requiring expensive equipment, significant insurance costs, and years of experience. It is not cheap, and shouldn't be. A crew with an aerial lift, a chipper, proper PPE, liability insurance, workers' comp, and certified arborists has real overhead.

The question isn't "who's cheapest" — it's "who's giving me the most value for what I'm spending." A well-maintained tree is worth significantly more than a damaged one, and a tree damaged by bad trimming can take a decade to recover — if it recovers at all.

## Santa Cruz-Specific Considerations

Santa Cruz County has a number of protected tree species and sizes — particularly oaks, redwoods, and trees above certain trunk diameter thresholds in incorporated areas. Unpermitted removal of protected trees can result in fines of **$20,000–$60,000** and mandatory replacement requirements.

A legitimate tree service will know the local regulations and tell you upfront if a permit is required. If a company suggests you don't need a permit for a large tree removal without checking, be skeptical.
