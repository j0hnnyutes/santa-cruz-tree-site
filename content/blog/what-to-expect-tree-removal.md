For most homeowners, a tree removal is an infrequent event. If you've never had one done, you might wonder what the process actually looks like, how long it takes, and what your property will look like afterward. Here's a straightforward walkthrough of what to expect.

## Before the Crew Arrives

A good tree removal company will have already walked your property before scheduling the removal — either in person or via photos — to scope the job. By the time the crew shows up, they should know:

- Which tree is being removed and the approach
- Whether a crane is needed
- Where equipment will stage and where debris will be moved
- Any specific concerns (power lines, overhead clearance, neighboring structures)

Make sure cars are moved out of the driveway and immediate work area. If there are items in the yard near the tree — furniture, decorations, garden beds — move them if possible, or point them out to the crew when they arrive.

## The Crew's Arrival and Setup

The crew will arrive with a truck, chipper, and any additional equipment (crane, aerial lift, etc.) the job requires. Before starting, they'll typically:

- Walk the site with you to confirm the scope and any concerns
- Position equipment — the chipper is usually staged near the truck, with hoses or ropes run to the tree
- Brief the crew on the plan

If a crane is involved, the crane operator will arrive separately and set up in a position that allows reach over the tree while keeping the crane on stable, accessible ground.

## The Removal Process

The specific method depends on the tree's size, location, and what's around it. Generally:

**For trees with open space around them:** The crew may fell the tree in sections or, for smaller trees, in one or two cuts if there's clearance to drop the tree in a safe direction.

**For trees near structures or with limited space:** This is the most common residential scenario. The climber ascends the tree, cuts branches and sections starting from the top down, and the rigging crew on the ground controls the descent of each piece using ropes. Sections are lowered carefully rather than dropped.

**For large trees requiring a crane:** The climber attaches a sling to a section, signals the crane operator, makes the cut, and the crane lifts the piece clear of the work area. This is slower than ground rigging but allows for very precise control of where each section goes.

As branches and sections come down, the ground crew feeds brush into the chipper and stacks or moves log sections as directed.

## How Long Does It Take?

This varies considerably:

- **Small tree (under 30 feet):** 1–3 hours
- **Medium tree (30–60 feet):** 2–5 hours
- **Large tree (60+ feet) near structures:** 4–8+ hours, sometimes more than one day
- **Very large crane removal:** Can take a full day or longer

If the stump is being ground on the same day (it's usually most efficient to do it while the crew is already there), add 30–90 minutes depending on stump size.

## During the Removal: What You Should Do

The safest place for you, your family, and pets is inside or well away from the work area. The crew needs to focus on the job, and bystanders — especially children and pets — near a removal can create dangerous distractions.

If you have questions or concerns, wait for a natural break in the work and speak with the crew lead rather than interrupting mid-cut.

## The Cleanup Process

After the tree is down, cleanup is an integral part of the job. A professional crew should:

- Run all brush through the chipper
- Stack or haul log rounds (confirm in advance whether rounds will be left or hauled)
- Rake the work area for small debris and wood chips
- Blow or rake the driveway and any adjacent areas that accumulated chips

Wood chip mulch from the chipper is a high-quality, free organic resource. Many homeowners ask the crew to leave the chips rather than haul them — they're excellent as garden mulch. Let the crew know beforehand if you want them.

What you'll likely be left with after cleanup:
- A clean yard with a stump at ground level (if stump grinding wasn't included)
- A small depression or chips in the area where the stump was ground (if grinding was included)
- Possibly some ruts from equipment in soft soil — experienced crews try to minimize this, especially in wet conditions

## After the Crew Leaves

A few things to know:

**Stump and surface roots:** If stump grinding wasn't part of the job, you'll have a stump remaining. This can be addressed later, or you can ask us to grind it at the time of removal (usually the most efficient option). Surface roots will remain in the ground and naturally decay over time.

**Settling:** The area where chips were left will settle over the following weeks as the material compacts. You can add topsoil and seed once it's settled.

**What to do with the space:** After a tree removal, you'll likely have more light in that area than you've had in years. It's a good opportunity to reassess your landscaping plans — whether that means a new planting, expanding a garden bed, or just enjoying the sunlight.

## Questions to Ask Before the Job

A few things worth confirming before any removal day:

- Who is responsible for stump grinding, and is it included in the quote?
- Will log rounds be hauled or left?
- What will the crew do with wood chips?
- Who should you call on the day of the job if there's an issue?

A professional company should be happy to answer all of these without hesitation.
