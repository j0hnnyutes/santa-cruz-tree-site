As Santa Cruz County emerges from the rainy season, trees are simultaneously showing the effects of winter storms and preparing for a season of vigorous growth. Spring — roughly February through April in our region — is one of the most important and most overlooked windows for tree care. What you do (or don't do) in spring can shape your trees' health, safety, and appearance for the rest of the year.

## Why Spring Tree Care Matters in Santa Cruz

Our Mediterranean climate makes spring tree care particularly high-value for a few reasons:

**Storm damage accumulation.** Every rainy season leaves behind some damage — cracked branches, split unions, soil erosion around root zones, and in worst cases, significant structural failures that aren't immediately obvious. Spring inspection catches these issues before summer heat and drought stress compound them.

**Pest and disease activity peaks in spring.** Several of the most significant tree threats in Santa Cruz County — including the bark beetles that vector Sudden Oak Death, aphid populations, and various fungal pathogens — ramp up dramatically as temperatures warm. Early identification means earlier intervention.

**Growth energy.** Trees are mobilizing stored energy from roots to support spring growth. Pruning done at the right time in spring can redirect that energy productively. Done at the wrong time, it can stress the tree or expose fresh wounds to pests during peak activity periods.

**Fire season preparation.** By late spring, the window to address fire risk before summer begins to close. Defensible space management, removal of dead material, and reduction of fire ladder fuels are all easier and less disruptive when done in early spring than in dry summer.

## Post-Winter Tree Inspection: What to Check

Walk your property in late February or early March — ideally after the last significant rain — and evaluate each tree systematically.

### Look for Storm Damage

Even if you didn't notice any dramatic failures, winter often leaves subtler damage:
- **Partially split branches** that are hanging or have secondary cracks at the base
- **Included bark unions** that have cracked under winter load — look for cracks at the v-junction of co-dominant stems
- **Soil movement** around the root zone — heaved or disturbed soil at the base of a tree can indicate root plate movement during a major storm
- **Fresh wounds** where bark was stripped or torn, which are potential entry points for disease

### Assess Canopy Condition

Spring is when dead branches become most visible — the tree is leafing out around them, making the lifeless wood stand out clearly. Look for:
- Branches with no leaf emergence while surrounding branches are actively leafing
- Brittle, gray, or peeling bark on branches that should be actively growing
- Dieback at the tips of branches that progresses toward the trunk

### Check for Pest and Disease Signs

**Sudden Oak Death on coast live oaks:** Look for dark, weeping patches of bark (usually lower trunk), wilting leaves, or recent leaf drop on an otherwise healthy-looking tree. SOD activity increases in wet spring conditions.

**Aphid populations:** Many ornamental trees — especially roses, flowering cherries, and young oaks — develop heavy aphid populations in spring. Sooty mold (black coating on leaves below aphid colonies) is a visible indicator.

**Pitch canker on pine:** Oozing resinous cankers on pine branches are often most visible in spring. Monterey pine on the coast and bishop pine in the mountains are particularly susceptible.

**Shot-hole fungus on stone fruit:** If you have flowering plum, cherry, or almond trees, check for small, circular holes in leaves — characteristic of *Wilsonomyces carpophilus*, common in Santa Cruz County after wet winters.

## Spring Pruning: What to Do and What to Avoid

### For Oaks: Wait Until August

This is the most important spring pruning rule in Santa Cruz County. **Do not prune coast live oaks from February through July.** This is peak bark beetle season, and the beetles that vector Sudden Oak Death are most active during warm spring months. Fresh pruning wounds act as entry points. Wait until late summer.

Exception: if a branch is actively failing or posing immediate safety risk, remove it and immediately apply pruning sealant to the cut surface to minimize infection risk.

### For Most Other Trees: Spring Is Fine

For non-oak trees, late winter through early spring — before full leaf-out — is actually an excellent pruning window. The tree's structure is visible, pests are just beginning to activate, and the growth energy of spring helps the tree compartmentalize wounds efficiently.

Specific spring priorities:
- **Remove storm damage:** Broken, hanging, and partially-failed branches
- **Prune fruit trees:** Late winter through early spring is the classic time for structural pruning of apples, pears, and citrus
- **Address suckers and water sprouts:** The vigorous growth of spring produces abundant suckers (growth from the root zone) and water sprouts (vertical shoots from major branches) — remove these early before they become significant
- **Thin young trees:** Spring is a good time to establish structure in young trees that are being trained

### Don't Over-Prune

Spring's vigorous growth tempts some homeowners to take off more than they should. The rule is consistent regardless of season: never remove more than 25% of the live crown in a single pruning cycle. More aggressive cuts stress the tree, produce abundant weak regrowth (water sprouts), and can expose the interior to sunscald once the dense canopy is thinned.

## Spring Fertilization

Trees emerging from winter can benefit from targeted nutrition, but not every tree needs fertilizing. Signs that fertilization may help:
- Pale or yellowing foliage (not from disease)
- Slow or minimal new growth compared to previous years
- Recovery from significant storm damage or pruning
- Young trees being established in poor soil

Santa Cruz County's coastal soils are often deficient in iron and manganese — elements that are important for leaf color and vigor in many tree species. A foliar analysis from a certified arborist can identify specific deficiencies before you apply anything.

**Avoid fertilizing stressed trees.** If a tree is under significant drought stress, disease pressure, or root zone damage, nitrogen fertilizer can stimulate growth that the tree's compromised root system can't support. Assess and address the underlying problem first.

## Spring Mulching

Spring is an excellent time to refresh mulch around trees. A 2–4 inch layer of organic mulch over the root zone:
- Retains moisture as the soil begins to dry
- Moderates soil temperature
- Suppresses competing weeds
- Improves soil biology over time

**Important:** Keep mulch at least 6 inches away from the trunk. Mulch piled against the bark (the "mulch volcano" pattern) retains moisture against the trunk, promotes rot, and creates pest habitat. The goal is to cover the root zone, not the trunk.

## Preparing for Fire Season

By April, it's time to start thinking about fire preparedness:
- Clear accumulated dead leaves, bark litter, and other combustibles from under trees
- Remove dead branches and deadwood throughout the canopy
- Eliminate "ladder fuels" — lower branches within 6–10 feet of the ground that would allow a ground fire to climb into the canopy
- Evaluate eucalyptus and pine trees for excessive bark debris accumulation
