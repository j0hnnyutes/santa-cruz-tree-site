A tree comes down in the middle of the night. It's on your roof, blocking your driveway, or resting against your fence. Your first question after making sure everyone is safe is almost always the same: how much is this going to cost?

Emergency tree service in Santa Cruz County is priced differently from scheduled work — and understanding how the pricing works helps you make smart decisions in a stressful moment.

## What "Emergency" Tree Service Actually Means

Emergency tree service covers situations where a tree or major limb has already failed and needs to be addressed immediately — usually because it's:

- On or against a structure (roof, wall, fence, car)
- Blocking a driveway, road, or emergency access
- On power lines or utility infrastructure
- Actively threatening to fall on an occupied area
- A major limb hanging partially broken ("widow maker") over a frequently used area

Situations that feel urgent but can safely wait 24–48 hours — a tree leaning after a storm that hasn't actually failed, dead branches you just noticed — are better scheduled as priority service rather than emergency response, which will save you money.

## Emergency Tree Service Costs in Santa Cruz County

Emergency tree service costs more than scheduled work for straightforward reasons: it requires immediate mobilization, often happens outside business hours, and frequently involves more complex and hazardous conditions (wet wood, low visibility, unstable soil, power line proximity).

**Typical emergency response costs in Santa Cruz County:**

- **Emergency call/mobilization fee:** $300–$600 (charged regardless of job size)
- **Tree or limb removal from structure (medium tree):** $1,500–$4,000
- **Tree or limb removal from structure (large tree):** $3,500–$8,000+
- **After-hours premium:** 25–50% above standard daytime rates
- **Weekend/holiday premium:** Often similar to after-hours

**What's typically included:**
- Immediate hazard mitigation (securing the tree/limb so it stops causing active damage)
- Removal of the tree or limb from the structure
- Basic cleanup and haul-away

**What may be billed separately:**
- Full debris cleanup beyond the immediate hazard
- Temporary tarping of damaged roof (often billed by the roofing contractor, not the tree service)
- Stump grinding (rarely done during emergency response)

## Factors That Drive Emergency Costs Up

**Power lines.** If the tree is in or near power lines, the tree service typically cannot proceed until PG&E has de-energized or isolated the lines. This may require coordination that delays the job and adds complexity — and PG&E's response time varies. Costs for line-clearance work are higher.

**Structural contact.** When a tree is actually resting on a roof, the crew must work slowly and carefully to remove weight progressively without causing additional structural damage. This takes significantly more time than removing a fallen tree from open ground.

**Tree size.** A 100-foot eucalyptus that came down is a much larger, more complex job than a 30-foot ornamental cherry. Very large trees on structures routinely run $6,000–$12,000 for emergency removal.

**Access.** If the tree is in a difficult-to-access location — steep hillside, narrow side yard, gated property — equipment options are limited and hand work takes longer.

**Time of night.** A call at 2 AM during a major storm costs more than a 7 AM call the next morning. If the situation can safely wait until daylight, waiting usually saves money.

## What Homeowner's Insurance Typically Covers

**Your homeowner's insurance generally covers:**
- Removal of a tree that has fallen on a covered structure (house, attached garage, fencing up to policy limits)
- Damage to the structure caused by the fallen tree
- Emergency mitigation costs

**What insurance typically does not cover:**
- Removal of a tree that fell in your yard but didn't hit a structure
- Removal of a tree from your neighbor's property that fell into your yard (that's your neighbor's insurance)
- Stump grinding
- Trees that were already dead or in known hazardous condition before the storm (potential claim denial)

**Document everything before cleanup starts.** Take extensive photos of the fallen tree, the point of failure, any structural damage, and the surrounding area. Your insurance adjuster will need this documentation, and it's much easier to capture before the job than after.

Call your insurance company promptly — most homeowner policies require timely notification of claims. Your insurer may have a preferred contractor list, but in most cases you are entitled to choose your own contractor.

## How to Avoid Getting Overcharged in an Emergency

Storm events attract "storm chasers" — out-of-area contractors who show up after major events, quote high prices, and may disappear before completing work. A few safeguards:

**Verify the license.** California tree work requires a contractor license. Ask for the license number and verify it at CSLB.ca.gov. A legitimate contractor will give you this without hesitation.

**Ask for a written scope before work starts.** Even in an emergency, a legitimate company will give you a written description of what they're doing and what it will cost. If someone won't put it in writing, that's a red flag.

**Get the mobilization fee and hourly rate in writing.** Understand whether you're being quoted a fixed price or a time-and-materials job. Fixed-price quotes are preferable for defined work (remove the tree from the roof). Time-and-materials is standard for cleanup, but get an estimated range.

**Don't sign over your insurance claim.** Some contractors ask homeowners to sign an "assignment of benefits" — essentially turning your insurance claim over to the contractor. This removes you from the process and can lead to inflated claims. Never sign this.
