Not all tree work requires a professional. A homeowner with basic tools can safely handle small pruning jobs — and there's real satisfaction in maintaining your own landscape. But tree work is among the most dangerous jobs in the country, and the line between "I can handle this" and "I need a professional" is worth understanding clearly.

## What You Can Safely Do Yourself

### Light Pruning on Small Trees

If you can reach the branch from the ground, the branch is no larger than 1.5 inches in diameter, and the tree is a manageable height, most homeowners can do basic pruning safely. Use clean, sharp bypass pruning shears or loppers, make cuts just outside the branch collar (the swollen area where the branch meets the trunk), and don't remove more than 25% of the canopy in a single season.

### Removing Small Dead Branches

Removing dead, diseased, or crossing branches from small ornamental trees or shrubs that don't require ladder work is generally within the DIY range.

### Picking Up Fallen Debris

After a storm or routine pruning, collecting and chipping small debris is safe and straightforward.

### Watering and Mulching

The best thing you can do for your trees is also one of the simplest: proper watering during dry months and a 3–4 inch layer of mulch around the base (keeping mulch away from the trunk). This dramatically improves tree health with no risk.

## When to Call a Professional

### Any Work Requiring a Ladder

Working above ground with a chainsaw is one of the most dangerous activities a person can do. Falls from ladders while operating a chainsaw are responsible for a disproportionate number of serious injuries and deaths each year. If the work requires you to climb, use a ladder, or operate a chainsaw overhead, hire a professional.

### Trees Near Power Lines

Never work near utility lines. Period. Even lines that appear to be insulated may not be fully protected, and contact can be fatal. Utility companies have specific protocols for tree work near lines, and so do professional arborists.

### Trees Near Structures

Removing or heavily trimming a tree near your house, fence, or neighbor's property requires careful rigging to control where branches and sections fall. An improperly cut branch can easily go through a window, punch a hole in a roof, or damage a fence. This is a job for someone who does it regularly.

### Large Branches and Canopy Work

Any branch larger than 3–4 inches in diameter creates significant weight and fall energy when cut. Improper cutting — especially cuts that don't account for the branch's tension and compression — can cause the branch to split and kick back, or to swing unpredictably. This requires training and experience to do safely.

### Hazardous, Dead, or Leaning Trees

If a tree is dead, significantly leaning, or appears structurally compromised, don't attempt any work on it yourself. The tree's behavior is unpredictable, and even a professional needs to carefully assess the situation before cutting.

### Work Requiring a Permit

Some tree removals in Santa Cruz County require permits. If your project needs a permit, it generally means the city or county considers the tree significant enough to regulate — which is also a signal that the work has elevated stakes.

## What Is a Certified Arborist?

A certified arborist is someone who has passed a comprehensive exam administered by the International Society of Arboriculture (ISA) and maintains their certification through continuing education. Certification demonstrates knowledge of tree biology, pruning techniques, pest identification, and safety practices.

An ISA-certified arborist is not the same as anyone who shows up with a chainsaw and a truck. The certification matters because it indicates a baseline of professional knowledge.

You can verify an arborist's certification on the ISA's website.

## What to Look for When Hiring a Tree Service

### Valid CA Contractor License

In California, tree work as a business requires a contractor's license (typically C-61/D-49 for tree service). You can verify licenses at the California Contractors State License Board (CSLB) website. An unlicensed contractor operating as a business is a red flag — and if something goes wrong, your recourse is limited.

### Proof of Insurance

Ask for a certificate of insurance showing:

- **General liability insurance:** Covers property damage during the job
- **Workers' compensation insurance:** Critical. If a worker is injured on your property and the company doesn't have workers' comp, you could be liable.

Any legitimate tree company will provide this without hesitation.

### Written Estimate with Scope Clearly Defined

A professional estimate should clearly state what's included: which trees or branches, debris removal, stump grinding or not. Verbal agreements leave room for disputes after the work is done.

### No Pressure to Decide Immediately

High-pressure sales tactics — "I can give you this price today only" — are common in fly-by-night tree operations. Take time to get multiple quotes and make a deliberate decision.

### Proper Equipment

Professional tree work requires proper equipment: chainsaws, rigging, chippers, climbing gear, and for larger jobs, cranes and boom lifts. A company that shows up with just chainsaws and ropes for a complex removal may not have the capacity to do the job safely.

## The Bottom Line

The honest answer is that most homeowners should hire a professional for anything beyond light pruning of small trees at ground level. The risk-reward calculation isn't favorable — tree work injuries are serious, property damage from poorly executed tree work is expensive, and the cost of professional service is usually quite reasonable relative to what's at stake.

When in doubt, get a professional opinion. A good arborist will tell you honestly what's necessary and what isn't — it shouldn't be a sales pitch for more work than you need.
