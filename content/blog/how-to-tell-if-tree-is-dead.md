Dead and dying trees are among the most common hazards on residential properties in Santa Cruz County — and one of the most underestimated. A tree that looks like it might be struggling can take years to fully die, but when it does fall, the damage is often catastrophic and preventable.

The challenge is that a dead tree and a dormant tree can look nearly identical. This guide will help you tell the difference and know when it's time to call a professional.

## The Scratch Test

The simplest way to check if a tree is alive is the scratch test. Find a small twig or young branch and use your fingernail or a knife to scratch through the outer bark layer. Just beneath the bark is a layer called the cambium.

- **Green or white-green tissue:** The tree is alive in that branch
- **Brown, dry, or fibrous tissue:** That branch is dead

Test multiple branches across the canopy. A tree may have some dead branches but still be alive overall — the key is whether the majority of the tree is viable.

## Signs a Tree Is Dying or Dead

### Bark That Falls Off

Healthy bark is firmly attached. When bark begins peeling, sloughing, or falling away from the trunk in large sections, it often signals the tree is dying or already dead. Look for bare, smooth, or discolored wood underneath.

### Cracks and Splits in the Trunk

Deep, vertical cracks in the main trunk are a red flag. They indicate structural weakness — the tree may have been struck by lightning, hit by a vehicle, or is experiencing internal rot. A cracked trunk can fail suddenly, especially during wind or rain.

### Fungal Growth at the Base

Mushrooms, shelf fungi (conks), or other fungal growth at the base of the trunk or on the roots are serious warning signs. Fungi typically grow in wood that is already rotting from within. A tree with significant fungal growth at the base may be structurally compromised even if the canopy still looks green.

### Sparse or Discolored Leaves

A healthy tree has a full, green canopy in season. Warning signs include:

- Leaves that are smaller than normal
- Yellowing, browning, or wilting leaves outside of fall
- Half the canopy has no leaves while the other half does
- Leaves that emerge, then die back in early summer ("tip dieback")

Any of these can indicate root problems, vascular disease, or significant stress.

### Dead Branches Concentrated at the Top

Dieback that starts at the top of the tree ("crown dieback") and works downward is a classic sign of decline. It can be caused by root damage, drought stress, disease, or pests. A tree where the top third of the canopy has dead branches should be evaluated by an arborist.

### Leaning Significantly

A sudden lean — one that wasn't present before — is an emergency sign. It can indicate root failure or trunk rot at the base. If a tree has recently started to lean, especially toward a structure or pathway, call a tree professional immediately.

### No Leaves in Spring

If a deciduous tree (one that normally loses leaves in winter) has no leaves by late spring, it's either dead or severely distressed. An evergreen that drops all of its needles or leaves out of season is also in serious trouble.

### Insect Infestation

Large amounts of wood-boring beetles, bark beetles, or other pests can indicate that a tree is already dying. These insects typically target stressed or dying trees. Frass (sawdust-like material) at the base of the trunk or boring holes in the bark are telltale signs.

## Dead vs. Dormant: How to Tell

Deciduous trees in winter can look dead when they're simply dormant. Before concluding a tree is dead, use the scratch test on multiple branches. Also look for:

- **Buds:** Even in winter, a living deciduous tree will have tight, firm buds on its branches. If you see no buds and the scratch test shows brown tissue throughout, the tree is likely dead.
- **Flexibility:** Live twigs bend without snapping. Dead twigs are brittle and snap cleanly.

## Is a Dead Tree Always Dangerous?

Not immediately, but eventually. A dead tree will begin to decay from the inside out — roots weaken, wood softens, and the tree becomes increasingly likely to fall. How fast this happens depends on the species and conditions. Some trees remain structurally sound for a few years after death; others degrade quickly.

Factors that increase risk:

- The tree is near a house, driveway, fence, or pedestrian path
- The tree has already lost major branches
- There has been recent wind, rain, or frost
- You can see fungal growth or visible rot

When in doubt, have the tree professionally assessed. The cost of an evaluation is a fraction of the cost of repairing a structure that's been hit by a falling tree.

## What to Do With a Dead or Dying Tree

1. **Get a professional assessment.** A certified arborist can tell you with confidence whether the tree is dead, how structurally compromised it is, and how urgent the removal is.
2. **Act before a storm.** Dead trees that fall during wind or rain cause far more damage than controlled removals. If a storm is coming and you have a dead tree near a structure, prioritize it.
3. **Check permit requirements.** In some parts of Santa Cruz County, even dead trees may require a removal permit. See our guide on tree removal permits.
4. **Consider the stump.** After removal, stump grinding prevents regrowth, eliminates trip hazards, and allows you to plant or landscape the area.
