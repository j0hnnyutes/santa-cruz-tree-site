California is one of the most regulated states in the country when it comes to trees on private property — and Santa Cruz County layers local protections on top of state requirements. Whether you're on a hillside parcel in the Santa Cruz Mountains, an urban lot in Scotts Valley, or a rural property in Corralitos, certain trees on your land are protected by law and cannot be removed without permits, documentation, and sometimes mitigation.

This guide covers tree protection rules in unincorporated Santa Cruz County, California state protections that apply everywhere, and how the rules differ in specific incorporated cities. Understanding the framework before you act can save you significant money, time, and legal exposure.

## Two Layers of Tree Protection: State and Local

Tree protection in California operates at two levels:

**State law** protects specific species and requires environmental review for certain large-scale projects. State protections apply regardless of local ordinances.

**Local ordinances** — adopted by cities and the county — add additional protections for trees based on size, species, location, or historical significance. Local rules are often more restrictive than state minimums.

Both layers can apply to a single tree removal decision.

## California State-Protected Tree Species

Several California tree species have state-level protections that apply throughout Santa Cruz County:

### Valley Oak (*Quercus lobata*)

Valley oak is one of California's most symbolically and ecologically important trees, and it receives strong legal protection. Under **California Public Resources Code Section 21083.4**, projects that would remove native oak woodlands must undergo environmental review under CEQA (California Environmental Quality Act). For private property owners, the practical implication is that removing valley oaks — particularly multiple trees or a grove — may require environmental impact analysis if the project is significant enough.

Valley oaks are most common in Santa Cruz County's inland and foothill areas — Corralitos, Pajaro Valley, and the eastern foothills near Gilroy.

### Coast Redwood (*Sequoia sempervirens*)

Coast redwood is protected under California law and under most local ordinances in Santa Cruz County. While there is no single blanket state law that prohibits all redwood removal, redwoods in timber production zones are regulated by the California Forest Practice Rules, and ornamental redwoods on residential properties are typically subject to local ordinance protections based on size.

In the Santa Cruz Mountains — Boulder Creek, Ben Lomond, Felton, and surrounding communities — mature redwoods on residential parcels are subject to Santa Cruz County's tree protection rules.

### Blue Oak (*Quercus douglasii*)

Blue oak receives similar protections to valley oak under California environmental law. Less common in Santa Cruz County than valley oak, but present in the drier inland foothills.

## Santa Cruz County Ordinance Protections (Unincorporated Areas)

For properties in unincorporated Santa Cruz County — meaning outside the incorporated city limits of Santa Cruz, Capitola, Scotts Valley, and Watsonville — the County's tree protection rules govern.

**The County's key thresholds:**

- **Coast live oak (*Quercus agrifolia*):** Protected trees typically require permits for removal. The County takes oak protection seriously given the density of coast live oak throughout the area and the Sudden Oak Death threat to remaining populations.

- **Valley oak and blue oak:** Protected as described under state law above.

- **Coast redwood:** Protected; removal permits required for trees meeting size thresholds.

- **California black walnut (*Juglans californica*):** A native species that receives protections in some County zones.

- **Size thresholds:** For other species, the County typically protects trees above a certain diameter (DBH). Check current County ordinance for the specific threshold, as this can be updated.

**How to check your parcel:** The Santa Cruz County Planning Department (831-454-2130) can tell you what protections apply to specific trees on your property. You can also research your parcel's zoning and any applicable overlay zones at the County's online permit and planning portal.

## Protected Trees in Santa Cruz County's Cities

Each incorporated city has its own ordinance:

### City of Santa Cruz

The City has a comprehensive tree protection ordinance covering "Significant Trees" (generally 10" DBH or larger) and designated Heritage Trees. See our detailed guide to the Santa Cruz City Tree Ordinance for the full breakdown.

### City of Capitola

Capitola's Heritage Tree Ordinance protects trees meeting specific size and significance criteria. The City requires permits for removal of protected trees and has replacement planting requirements. Contact the Capitola Community Development Department for current specifics.

### City of Scotts Valley

Scotts Valley has tree protection policies focused primarily on significant native species. Contact the City's Planning Division for current requirements before removing any large or native tree within city limits.

### City of Watsonville

Watsonville has tree protection requirements particularly focused on the agricultural and natural resource zones. Protected species and size thresholds apply. The Watsonville Planning Department can advise on specific parcels.

## What Happens If You Remove a Protected Tree Without a Permit

The consequences of unpermitted removal of protected trees in Santa Cruz County can be severe:

**Fines.** Fines for unpermitted removal of protected trees can be calculated as a multiple of the tree's appraised value. A mature coast live oak appraised at $15,000–$50,000 (using standard arboricultural appraisal methods) can result in fines of $30,000–$150,000 for unpermitted removal.

**Replacement requirements.** Beyond fines, jurisdictions typically require replacement planting — sometimes at ratios of 3:1 or higher for unpermitted removals, compared to 1:1 for permitted removals.

**Stop-work orders.** If protected tree removal is associated with a construction project, the entire project may receive a stop-work order until the tree issue is resolved.

**Enforcement.** Both the City and County receive complaints about unpermitted removals from neighbors. Aerial photography is increasingly used to document pre/post conditions. Enforcement is real.

## Practical Steps Before Removing Any Large Tree

1. **Identify the species.** If you don't know what species your tree is, have an arborist identify it. Species determines whether state protections apply.

2. **Measure the trunk diameter.** Measure circumference at 4.5 feet above ground, then divide by π (3.14) to get diameter. This tells you whether the tree meets local size thresholds.

3. **Contact the relevant planning department.** City or County, depending on where your parcel is. Describe the tree and ask whether a permit is required.

4. **Get an arborist report.** For any tree that requires a permit, you'll almost certainly need a written report from an ISA Certified Arborist documenting the condition of the tree and the basis for the removal recommendation.

5. **Apply for the permit.** Your arborist or tree service can often assist with the application.

6. **Only then schedule removal.** Work with a licensed contractor who understands the permit conditions (replacement planting timing, species, size requirements).
